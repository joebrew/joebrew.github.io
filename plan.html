This document describes Joe Brew’s PhD programme, both in terms of (a) the research he is doing and (b) the training he is receiving.

Research

Questions

Joe Brew’s research addresses important questions about both the impact, the challenges, costs and benefits of malaria, its control and elimination. Specifically, his research aims to produce the following knowledge products:

Papers

  • Effectiveness of a malaria elimination campaign in Southern Mozambique using routine surveillance data.
  • Question: What is the effect of a malaria elimination campaign in a district of Southern Mozambique?
  • Hypothesis:The elimination campaign reduced malaria cases significantly in the short-term.
  • Data: Routine surveillance data from the Ministry of Health; Program data on costs, implementation area; Demographic data from census.

  • The short term educational impact of a malaria elimination campaign in Southern Mozambique.
  • Question: What is the non-health impact (absenteeism and academic achievement) of a malaria elimination campaign among schoolchildren in Southern Mozambique?
  • Hypothesis: The malaria elimination campaign brought about a reduction in absenteeism and an improvement in school grades among students in the elimination zone, relative to those outside of the elimination zone.
  • Absenteeism and performance data from 9 schools, 5 in a malaria elimination zone, and the other 4 in a malaria control zone; Demographic data from census.

  • The private economic return of malaria control: the case of sugarcane workers in Southern Mozambique.
  • Question: Which is the economic return of malaria control carried out by a sugar cane company?
  • Hypothesis: Investment in malaria control has a strong impact and offers a high return in terms of reduced workers’ absenteeism and increased workers’ productivity.
  • Data: Absenteeism and productivity information from a large sugarcane factory in Southern Mozambique based in a malaria control zone; Private malaria control program costs and activity data; Private worker clinic data.

  • The short term economic impact of a malaria elimination campaign: the case of sugarcane workers in Southern Mozambique.
  • Question: Which is the short term economic impact in terms of sugar cane workers’ absenteeism and productivity of a malaria elimination campaign in Southern Mozambique?
  • Hypothesis: Malaria elimination sensibly improves absenteeism and productivity indicators in comparison with malaria control.  
  • Data: Absenteeism and productivity information from two large sugarcane factories in Southern Mozambique, one based in a malaria elimination zone and the other based in a malaria control zone; Private malaria control program costs and activity data; Private worker clinic data.

  • Can we do it? A survey of malaria research professionals on the likelihood of and timeframe to global malaria eradication.
  • Question: What are the perceived feasibility and the expected value of global malaria eradication, and how does it compare to control?
  • Hypothesis: Malaria researchers’ opinions regarding the likelihood of and time-frame to malaria eradication can be aggregated to estimate true probability, and are therefore useful in order to approximate expected economic value.
  • Data: Nearly 1,000 survey responses pertaining to  from researchers who have published peer-reviewed research on malaria.

  • Foreign direct investment, corporate social responsibility, and malaria control in Mozambique (paper close to submission).
  • Question: What strategies and funding mechanisms are feasible for scaling up malaria control and elimination, specifically from the perspective of the private sector?
  • Hypothesis: The private sector, specifically foreign direct investment and corporate social responsibility, offers a promising and underutilized mechanism by which malaria control can be scaled up in a sustainable - and in some cases profitable - fashion.
  • Data: Aggregate-level census and economic data, collected and maintained by outside institutions (National Statistics Institute of Mozambique, World Bank, International Monetary Fund, World Health Organization); Literature review of both academic and “grey” sources.

  • Malaria immunisation across Africa: Estimating the costs of implementation (paper close to submission).
  • Question: What future costs can malaria elimination campaigns expect to encounter, specifically in terms of the implementation costs associated with vaccine administration?
  • Hypothesis: Assuming the development of a safe and effective malaria vaccine in the near future, administration costs can be estimated based on the program costs of historical vaccination campaigns.
  • Data: Collected in 2016 via a systematic review of academic literature pertaining to vaccine administration program costs.
